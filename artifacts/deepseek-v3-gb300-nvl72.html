DeepSeek-V3 inference in five charts

A 671-billion-parameter mixture-of-experts model is what a GB300 NVL72 rack is actually for — and it bends every rule of dense-model serving. The KV cache is compressed a hundredfold but refuses to shard the usual way; the weights don't fit a chip, so parallelism is mandatory before it is fast; and the expert bank turns batch size from a discount lever into the whole business.

Every number here comes from inferencesim's roofline engine. The reference deployment is DeepSeek-V3 on a GB300 NVL72 rack at tp=1, ep=8 — TRT-LLM's DEP8: attention runs data-parallel across 8 chips while the 256 experts shard over them — with fp4 weights, fp8 KV, 4k-token prompts, 1k-token outputs, batch 256. Each chart varies one axis. (A companion page runs the same five studies on dense Llama-3.1-70B.)

These are speed-of-light numbers. Perfect tiling, 100%-efficient bandwidth, bandwidth-optimal collectives, zero kernel overhead, and expert routing with no skew — every value is an optimistic bound. The shapes and crossovers are the lesson, not the absolute values.

1 · The two phases, remixed

MLA flips the roofline

For a dense GQA model, the rule is simple: prefill is compute-bound, decode streams from memory. DeepSeek's multi-head latent attention breaks it. The KV cache is one shared 576-dim latent per layer instead of per-head keys and values, so attention reads almost nothing from DRAM while still doing 128 heads of math — and even decode attention crosses the ridge into compute-bound territory. The memory burden moves to the routed experts, which must stream every expert any token picked.

Why it matters: MLA buys its hundred-fold KV compression by re-spending FLOPs — a trade that only works on chips with compute to spare, exactly what dense decode leaves idle. And because the memory wall moves from attention to expert streaming, the fix is no longer "more KV bandwidth" but "more batch" (chart 5) and "more expert shards" (chart 2).

2 · The EP ladder

Expert parallelism is mandatory before it is fast

At fp4, DeepSeek-V3's weights are 335 GB — no single 288 GB chip can even hold them, so ep=1 is not a slow option, it is not an option. From there, every doubling of the EP group shards the expert bank and the MLA-batch further, and unlike dense TP (two ring allreduces per layer), the MoE all-to-all's payload also shrinks with ep — so the ladder never turns against you on latency. What grows instead is the bill.

Why it matters: for dense models TP eventually turns latency worse (the KV-head wall); for MoE, EP keeps buying latency all the way up — you stop because of economics, not physics. Real deployments pick the rung by SLA: ep=8 here gives 123 tok/s/user at $0.42/M with zero idle silicon, which is why DEP8-class configs are common; ep=64 is what a latency-at-any-price product buys.

3 · What EP shards

EP divides what TP cannot touch

Parallelism dimensions aren't interchangeable — each one shards a specific set of bytes. For DeepSeek, TP slices the matmuls but cannot shard the MLA cache at all: the compressed latent has no heads to split, so every TP rank keeps a full replica. EP is the opposite: it splits the expert bank (the bulk of 671B) and, because attention runs data-parallel across the EP group, it divides the KV cache by batch-sharding.

Why it matters: "just add TP" — the dense reflex — does nothing for an MLA model's memory problem. Deployment shape is dictated by which bytes are the constraint: expert weights and KV say ep, the per-layer matmuls say tp, and the replicated floor says even infinite ep leaves ~8.5 GB per chip untouched.

4 · Batch economics

The discount runs deeper for MoE

Dense batching amortizes one fixed weight stream, so its discount is spent by batch ≈ 64. MoE keeps a second amortization running: each doubling of batch also spreads the ever-growing union of activated experts over more tokens, so the price keeps falling long after a dense curve has flattened. And with tp=1 there is no allreduce latency floor under any of it.

Why it matters: the marginal value of batch is architecture-dependent. A dense fleet can stop growing batches at the SLA knee; an MoE fleet leaves real money on the table unless it aggregates traffic until the expert pool saturates — which is why MoE serving gravitates to fewer, larger, busier deployments.

5 · Expert amortization

256 experts, and every one of them reads

Each DeepSeek token activates 8 of 256 experts — but which 8 differs per token, so a decode step streams the union. At batch 1 that's 8 experts' weights for one token; at batch 256, essentially all 256 for 256 tokens. Expert traffic per token therefore falls as E(B)/B, not 1/B — the curve that justifies this page's entire deployment shape.

Why it matters: this curve, times chart 2's ladder, is the DeepSeek serving playbook — shard experts wide (ep), aggregate the biggest batch traffic allows, and let attention ride data-parallel on MLA's tiny cache. It's also why MoE benchmarks at batch 1 flatter the architecture: the expert stream is nearly free there and brutal at mid-batch.
