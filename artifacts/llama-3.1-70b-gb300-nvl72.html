inferencesim · five roofline studies

LLM inference in five charts

Why does a rack that prefills at petaflops crawl through decode? Why does doubling tensor parallelism sometimes make latency worse? Why do MoE models only make economic sense at large batch? The answers are arithmetic, and arithmetic can be drawn.

Every number on this page was computed by inferencesim, a bottom-up simulator of LLM inference performance. The charts share one reference deployment — Llama‑3.1‑70B on a GB300 NVL72 rack (tp=8, fp4 weights, fp8 KV cache, 4k‑token prompts, 1k‑token outputs, batch 64) — and each chart varies exactly one axis of it.

These are speed-of-light numbers. The roofline engine assumes perfect tiling, 100%-efficient bandwidth, bandwidth-optimal collectives, and zero kernel overhead — every value is an optimistic bound that real systems approach but don't reach. The shapes and crossovers are the lesson, not the absolute values.

1 · The two phases

Prefill computes, decode streams

An op's arithmetic intensity — FLOPs performed per byte moved from DRAM — decides which hardware limit it hits. Below the ridge point (peak FLOP/s ÷ memory bandwidth) an op is memory-bound: the math units wait on HBM. Above it, compute-bound. Plotting every op of one transformer layer against the chip's roofline shows the two serving phases live on opposite sides of that line.

Why it matters: one phase wants FLOPs, the other wants bandwidth — served on the same chips, each wastes what the other needs. This asymmetry is the entire case for prefill/decode disaggregation (DistServe, NVIDIA Dynamo), and why "how fast is this GPU for inference?" has no single answer.

2 · The TP tax

Tensor parallelism buys latency — until it doesn't

TP shards every weight matrix across chips, so each chip streams 1/tp of the bytes per token — but stitches the result back together with two ring allreduces per layer (160 per decode step for this 80-layer model). Weight streaming shrinks like 1/tp; the collective grows with tp. Somewhere those curves cross.

Why it matters: TP is the latency knob, but its budget is set by the model's KV-head count and the fabric's latency — not by how many chips you own. The remaining chips are better spent on data-parallel replicas (throughput) or, past the KV wall, on attention-data-parallelism (chart 3). One honesty note: the engine prices every allreduce with the ring closed form even on NVL72's switched fabric, so the comm growth here is a conservative bound.

3 · KV-cache anatomy

The KV cache is an architectural choice

Every decoded token must re-read the KV cache of everything before it, and every running request holds its cache in HBM — so the bytes-per-token growth rate set by the attention design at pretraining time becomes serving's rent. The three modern designs pay very different rents, and shard very differently.

Why it matters: KV capacity bounds batch size, and batch size is the economics (chart 4). A model's attention design decides whether long-context serving is a memory problem (GQA), a solved problem (MLA), or a hybrid (SWA) — and which parallelism dimension can rescue it.

4 · Batch economics

Batch size is the price lever

In decode, the weights stream from HBM once per step no matter how many requests share the step. Batching divides that fixed cost across users — while each user's own tokens arrive a little slower. This one curve is the business model of every inference provider.

Why it matters: throughput is manufactured by batching away interactivity — which is why the same model costs 30× more with a strict latency SLA, why providers overbook decode batches, and why the KV capacity that bounds the batch (chart 3) is worth real money.

5 · MoE amortization

MoE only pays at batch

A mixture-of-experts model activates a few experts per token — but which few differs per token, so a decode step must read every expert any token picked. At batch 1 that's top-k experts; as the batch grows, the union approaches all of them. Expert weight traffic therefore falls not like dense's 1/B, but like E(B)/B — the expected distinct experts divided by the batch.

Why it matters: this curve is why MoE deployments chase huge global batches with expert parallelism — shard the experts over an EP group, run attention data-parallel, and let the combined batch saturate the expert pool so every chip amortizes its shard. It is also why MoE looks deceptively cheap in single-stream benchmarks and disappointingly expensive at low traffic.
